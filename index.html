Read Sat 7/18, on the drive in1 · Lassen — the volcano that blew up on camera

In 1914, Lassen Peak had been quiet so long that locals picnicked on it. Then, on May 30, it cleared its throat — and for the next three years put on the American West's only 20th-century volcanic eruption south of Alaska until St. Helens.

Lassen is a geologic sampler platter: one of the few places on Earth with all four volcano types in one park — shield (Prospect Peak), composite/stratovolcano (Brokeoff, the eroded remnant of ancient Mount Tehama), cinder cone (the aptly named Cinder Cone), and plug dome. Lassen Peak itself is one of the largest plug-dome volcanoes in the world: a ~27,000-year-old bulge of stiff dacite lava that squeezed up like toothpaste too thick to flow away.

The 1914–17 show peaked on May 22, 1915, when the summit exploded: an avalanche of hot rock and melting snow tore down the northeast flank, followed by a lateral blast that flattened trees three miles out and lofted ash that fell 280 miles away. Local businessman B.F. Loomis photographed the eruptions with a homemade telephoto setup — his images helped convince Congress to create the national park in August 1916. You'll drive straight through the scar: the Devastated Area, where a century of regrowth shows as a young, oddly uniform forest among blast boulders — a natural lab for how conifers recolonize a wiped slate.

Bumpass Hell (your morning hike) is the park's largest hydrothermal area — ~16 acres of boiling springs, mudpots, and fumaroles. The plumbing: snowmelt seeps down ~1–2 miles to rock cooked by magma, flashes to steam, and roars back up. Big Boiler, the main fumarole, has been measured above 300°F — among the hottest steam vents on Earth. The name is a warning label: guide Kendall Bumpass broke through the thin crust in the 1860s and lost his leg to a boiling pool. Stay on the boardwalk; the ground is literally a lid.

Read Sun 7/19 morning2 · Mt Shasta & the birth of the Sacramento

The bottle you fill at Mt Shasta City Park this morning holds snow that fell on the mountain decades ago — filtered through a volcano and emerging as the headwaters spring of California's largest river.

Mount Shasta (14,179 ft) is the giant of the southern Cascades — a stratovolcano so massive it's among the most voluminous in the entire arc, visible from 100+ miles on a clear day. It's really a pile of overlapping cones built over hundreds of thousands of years; the youngest, Shastina, on its west shoulder, would be a major peak on its own. Shasta still hosts several glaciers — including Whitney Glacier, the longest in California — and it is dormant, not dead: its last eruption was within the past ~800 years, and USGS ranks it among the highest-threat volcanoes in the country (which mostly means it's very well monitored).

At City Park, cold water pours out of a lava-rock hillside at Big Springs. Snowmelt percolates into the volcano's porous rock and travels underground — residence-time estimates run to decades — before surfacing here, celebrated as the headwaters of the Sacramento River: from this trickle to San Francisco Bay, ~400 miles.

The mountain has been sacred far longer than it's been climbed. For the Winnemem Wintu ("Middle Water People" of the McCloud River), Shasta is central to their origin — they emerged from a sacred spring on the mountain, and it remains a place of ceremony and obligation, as it is in different ways for the Shasta, Karuk, Modoc, and Pit River peoples. The Winnemem Wintu today lead efforts to return winter-run Chinook salmon to the McCloud, blocked since Shasta Dam (1945) flooded much of their homeland. Fill your bottle with that in mind: this is borrowed water from a mountain a lot of people consider alive.

Read Sun 7/19 afternoon — passenger only, 55 hairpins3 · Hwy 299: gold, the Trinity, and a temple in the forest

The highway you're winding along follows the Trinity River, and nearly every gravel bar you pass was turned over by hand, by hose, or by dredge in one of California's most intense gold rushes.

Gold hit the Trinity River in 1848, months after Sutter's Mill, and by 1850 Weaverville existed as a supply camp deep in the mountains. What makes Trinity County's story distinctive is who did much of the mining: by the mid-1850s roughly 2,000 Chinese miners lived and worked around Weaverville — reworking claims, running stores, and building a community despite discriminatory taxes and violence (including the infamous 1854 "Weaverville Tong War," an armed clash between rival Chinese companies fought as a spectacle while white miners watched).

Their most enduring mark is the Weaverville Joss House — "The Temple Amongst the Forest Beneath the Clouds" — rebuilt in 1874 after fire, still holding its original altar brought from China: the oldest continuously used Chinese temple in California, now a State Historic Park. (You're skipping the Weaverville stop this trip, but it's visible history for a future pass.)

The mining escalated to hydraulic mining — water cannons hosing entire hillsides into sluices. The La Grange Mine west of Weaverville was among the largest hydraulic operations in the world; 299 passes its raw, still-unhealed scar near Oregon Mountain. Later, floating dredges flipped the riverbed into neat cobble rows you can still spot from the road. Today the Trinity is better known as a premier salmon and steelhead river — central to Hupa and Yurok fisheries downstream where it joins the Klamath. Watch for rafters below the pullouts.

Read Eureka week, before your first grove4 · Coast redwoods: fog-drinkers, fire-survivors, and the ones that got away

The tallest living things on Earth exist in a strip of coast about 450 miles long and rarely 30 miles wide — and the reason is the same fog that will be sitting on your Airbnb half the mornings this week.

Coast redwood (Sequoia sempervirens) is the tallest tree species on the planet — the record tree, Hyperion (in Redwood NP; location closed to visitors), tops 380 feet. They live 2,000+ years. Their tricks:

  • Fog drip. Summer here is rainless, but redwoods comb water out of marine fog with their needles; fog drip and direct foliar uptake supply a large share of their summer water — which is why the tallest groves sit in fog-funneling river flats like Prairie Creek.
  • Fire armor. Bark up to a foot thick, low in resin, high in tannins — mature trees shrug off most fires. The blackened, hollowed "goose-pen" trees you'll pass are survivors, not casualties.
  • Clone-sprouting. Cut or burn one and it resprouts from the base — the circular "fairy rings" you'll see are genetically identical shoots around a vanished parent.

Before 1850, ~2 million acres of old-growth redwood ran Big Sur to southern Oregon. The Gold Rush, the post-1906 rebuilding of San Francisco, and industrial logging left about 5% standing — nearly half of it inside Redwood National and State Parks, the patchwork you're exploring. The rescue began in 1918 with Save the Redwoods League buying groves outright — often via "memorial grove" donations (hence all the family names). The state parks, Prairie Creek among them, date to the 1920s; Redwood National Park came only in 1968, expanded in 1978 around logged-over Redwood Creek watershed still being restored today. In the Fern Canyon / James Irvine forest on Friday, you're standing in the heart of what the League's founders were racing to save.

Read on an Old Town Eureka evening5 · Wigi / Humboldt Bay: Wiyot, Yurok, Tolowa — and a rare piece of land returned

The island you can see from the Eureka boardwalk is the center of the world — literally, in Wiyot cosmology — and in 2019 it became one of the first places a U.S. city ever returned to a tribe, no strings attached.

Humboldt Bay is Wiyot homeland — their name for the bay is Wigi. Tuluwat, on Indian Island opposite Old Town, is the site of the Wiyot world-renewal ceremony, held each year to bring the world back into balance. In February 1860, during that ceremony, Eureka-area settlers crossed to the island at night and massacred the Wiyot sleeping there — mostly women, children, and elders; coordinated attacks hit other villages the same night. The young writer Bret Harte published a furious denunciation and was run out of the region for it. Survivors were interned; the Wiyot were nearly destroyed.

The modern coda is why this is worth reading on the waterfront: the Wiyot Tribe bought back 1.5 acres of Tuluwat in 2000, restored it from an industrial site, and in 2019 the City of Eureka returned over 200 acres of Indian Island to the Tribe — a nearly unprecedented unconditional municipal land return. In 2014, the world-renewal ceremony was completed on Tuluwat for the first time in 154 years.

North of the bay you're in Yurok country — the largest tribe in California today, centered on the lower Klamath. Redwood was their building material (plank houses, seagoing dugout canoes carved from fallen logs); salmon their economy. The Yurok Tribe now co-manages parts of Redwood National and State Parks, reintroduced California condors to the region in 2022, and is receiving back land near Orick ('O Rew) at the parks' gateway. Farther north around the Smith River are the Tolowa Dee-ni', who suffered their own massacres in the 1850s (Yontocket). Every grove you walk this week is on land these nations never stopped calling home.

Read Sat 7/25, return leg6 · Cascadia: the coast that's loading a spring

The wave-battered coast you're driving home along is the most seismically active corner of the lower 48 — and the sea stacks, marine terraces, and tsunami-zone signs all tell the same story.

Offshore, the small Gorda plate (southern end of the Juan de Fuca system) is diving beneath North America along the Cascadia Subduction Zone. Near Cape Mendocino — just south of the Lost Coast — it meets the Pacific plate and the San Andreas fault at the Mendocino Triple Junction, a three-plate pileup that makes Humboldt County California's earthquake capital (locals barely blink at magnitude 5s; a 7.0 struck offshore as recently as December 2024).

The zone's signature move is the megathrust rupture. The last full one hit on January 26, 1700 — dated to the evening hour, remarkably, by combining Native oral histories, drowned "ghost forests" of cedar killed by sudden coastal subsidence, and Japanese records of an "orphan tsunami" that arrived with no felt earthquake. Likely magnitude ~9. The southern (your) end has the shortest average reload interval — which is why every beach town has blue tsunami evacuation signs, and why Crescent City, hit fatally by the 1964 Alaska-quake tsunami, takes them seriously.

You can read the uplift directly in the landscape: the King Range of the Lost Coast rises so fast (a few mm per year — glacially fast) that Highway 1's engineers gave up and detoured inland, creating California's longest roadless coastline. Flat benches perched above the surf are marine terraces — old wave-cut sea floors hoisted up by successive quakes. And the sea stacks at Trinidad, Elk, and Point Arena are the rearguard of retreating cliffs: hard knobs the ocean hasn't finished demolishing. The prettiest stretches of this drive are, geologically speaking, wreckage from a fight that's still going.

Flora & fauna — what to spot

Two very different worlds this trip — volcanic high country, then the fog forest and coast. Keep an eye out for these.

Mountains — Lassen (Sat 7/18)

Marmots & pikas Yellow-bellied marmots sun themselves on the rock piles around Bumpass Hell; listen for the pika's high "eep!" from the talus. Pikas don't hibernate — a July sighting is easy.
Clark's nutcracker Noisy gray-black-and-white bird at treeline, cousin of the jays — it caches thousands of pine seeds and remembers where.
Mule deer & Steller's jay Deer graze the meadows at dawn/dusk; the dark, crested blue Steller's jay works the picnic areas (don't feed it).
Wildflowers & thermophiles July meadows: lupine, Indian paintbrush, mule-ears. And the greens/oranges tinting Bumpass Hell's pools are heat-loving microbes, alive in near-boiling water — not just minerals.

Redwoods & coast (Eureka week + return)

Banana slug The forest's bright-yellow mascot and chief recycler — out on damp trails after the fog. Bright color warns of a numbing slime; look, don't taste.
Roosevelt elk Big herds at Elk Prairie and Gold Bluffs Beach — the largest elk in North America. Keep 25+ yards Bulls get aggressive as summer rut builds; never get between a cow and calf.
Marbled murrelet An endangered little seabird that nests only in old-growth canopy — the reason parks beg you not to feed jays/ravens, which raid its nests. You won't see it; now you know why the "no food" signs are serious.
California condor North America's largest bird (~9.5 ft span) — reintroduced to this region by the Yurok Tribe starting 2022, the first condors here in over a century. Long shot, but scan the sky at Klamath River Overlook. Numbered wing tags = a known bird.
Harbor seals & tide pools Seals haul out around Trinidad; low-tide rocks hold sea stars, anemones, and hermit crabs (though this week's good lows are pre-dawn — see Timing).
Black bear Present but shy in the redwoods — store food, don't leave a cooler out at the Airbnb or trailheads.
Wildlife etiquette: give room — 25+ yards from elk, farther from bears. Never feed anything (corvids included — they hunt murrelet nests). Stay on trails, pack out all food, and keep the Airbnb bear-smart.

Stargazing

Sat 7/18 verdict: very good night (verified, USNO). Waxing crescent only 24% lit, hanging low in the west — minor glow, actually a nice photo element over the lake. Moonset 11:06p → fully moon-free after that. Astronomical darkness starts 10:31p (Bortle ~2 skies out there).
  • Primary: Manzanita Lake, Sat 7/18, 8:50–9:40p block — bright stars, the Summer Triangle (Vega/Deneb/Altair) overhead, and the Milky Way core just emerging in the south over the lake (core rises over/near Lassen Peak early in the night).
  • The upgrade: the core turns naked-eye obvious ~9:45–10p and is best from 10:30p onward once fully dark. If you can stretch to ~10:30–11:15p before the ~50 min drive back, you get the real show — core + reflection, crescent setting. Weigh against the Sunday drive day.
  • Also up: Saturn rises late evening in the east (best after ~11p) · possible early Perseid stragglers (peak Aug 12). Check Stellarium/Sky Guide day-of for exact planet positions.
  • Face south/southeast over the lake for the core.
  • Eureka week (secondary): moon hits first quarter 7/21 → waxing gibbous by 7/24–25, up until ~midnight–2a — each night progressively worse. Combined with marine-layer fog, treat coastal stargazing as a bonus, not a plan. If a clear early-week night appears (Mon/Tue best moon-wise): Trinidad / College Cove pullouts or Klamath Overlook, looking south.
  • Kit: red-light headlamp mode · warm layers + beanie (~5,900 ft — 40s°F after dark even in July) · insect repellent (lakeside mosquitoes) · tripod + widest lens; phone night mode handles the core fine · let eyes adapt ~15 min.
